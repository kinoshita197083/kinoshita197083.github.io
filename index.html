Is Covid just a flu? If not, will it be?

The year of 2019 has been a life changing year

And it will surely occupy at least a page in future literatures. The debuting of COVID has brought significant impacts to the world, though, some may see it as a marvellous opportunity while some may see it as a catastrophe. The attitude of a sovereign, or even a nation, determine its behavioural decisions as well as its countermeasure towards COVID to a great extent. Speaking of national level of point of view, each nation’s strategies in manging the pandemic largely varied. In essence, it is a dilemma of putting either the economy or social stability as the top priority at that one specific timeframe. “It’s just a flu” has been a famous quote of former president of the U.S. – Donald Trump during the initial stage of the pandemic. This data exploration study attempts to examine such statement, to see whether it was just a soothing statement or in fact, it was a scientific conclusion.

Global Death Toll of Covid-19 has Exceeded 6 million

Global Situation

Globally, there have been more than 600 million of confirmed cases of COVID-19, which has been declared as a pandemic on 11 March 2020 by World Health Organisation (WHO). After more than a decade from the H1N1 Swine Flu (2009), the last global pandemic, people, and especially, politicians often make comparisons between the two. Although, former is categorised as influenza virus while the latter is corona virus, still, they share many similarities. Not only the contagiousness and respiratory symptoms, but also the evolution processes as well as the way how human deal with it. Therefore, it will be intriguing to take both into examination and see if we could find something that we could not have thought of.

Case Fatality Rate Comparison between 2009 Pandemic & COVID-19

Fatality can be simply referred to the death rate per 100 cases of an infectious disease. When performing statistical analysis of fatality, there have been three mainstream approaches including Case Fatality Rate (CFR), Infectious Fatality Rate (IFR) and Crude Mortality Rate. CFR has been a popular approach and often cited by media when risk of death of COVID-19 is discussed; It is calculated through “number of death / total of diagnosed cases * 100“ while IFR is calculated as “number of death / total number of infected * 100”. Ideally, IFR seems to be the best approach to answer the question of “how deadly the disease is ?”, however, total number of infected is often not known especially for COVID-19 as there are many people who were symptomless and thus had not been diagnosed as a case. Therefore, CFR becomes a reasonable choice for media as well as the current study in comparing the fatality rate of the diseases.

In terms of CFR, Trump could be right

The study first addressed the issue of multiple approaches of calculating death rate and have been used inconsistently by media, politicians, and researchers. The concepts of Case Fatality Rate (CFR), Infection Fatality Rate (IFR), and Crude Mortality Rate were then introduced and declared CFR will be employed as the base comparing approach between diseases along with the reasons of why such approach. As the result, research question 1 was answered through comparing the CFR between H1N1 swine flu (2009) and COVID-19, which supports Trump’s statement and verified that CFR was the approach of his conclusions. In the exploration of the CFR, the seasonal flu, as the extensions of H1N1 flu, was found highly resembling COVID-19 and thus, the health-care system burden of seasonal flu was examined. An interesting pattern was later found and happened to match with the current state of COVID-19. Based on such finding, a hypothesis was proposed inferring that COVID-19 will be very likely to have a significant bounce back in the upcoming quarter or year.

Reflection

In researching the current project, I learnt the concepts of CFR, CMR and IFR. It assisted me to developed analyses that were so much more in-depth and I like such mindset a great deal since I am a psychology graduated. The understanding of different statistical approaches allow clarifications of the puzzle of fatality, which often large varied across nations but we tend to ascribe the reason to economical factors. Media often cited the CFR approach while institutional researches usually employed IFR as they are capable to develop statistical modelling via machine learning and thus, able to estimate the total number of infected with a relatively reasonable amount of errors. As for this study, it is worth noting that the theoretical thoughts were proposed solely based on the healthcare-system burden datasets adapted from U.S. CDC (2020). CFR was employed in exploration due to the absence of data modelling capability. The research approach was to “analyse what I see not what I think”, therefore, the inference may seem to be reasonable to certain degree, but it was not rigorous and thorough. It required large amount of further researches to refine and add complexities such as territory, age and chronic illness. One of the major limitations is that the datasets were limited to the U.S. providing limited representativeness. Data of H1N1 swine flu in 2009, in particular, was almost not possible to be recovered without adapting existing estimations unlike data of COVID-19. When comparing the two disease, age was actually another intriguing factor, which would be even more interesting if it was included into the data explorations.
